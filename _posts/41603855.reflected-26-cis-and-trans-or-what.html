Today, I’d like to shed some more light on the diagram we saw a couple of emails ago. More specifically, we’ll focus on the combinations that emerge from the green arrows:

Last time, we dived deeper into gender identity and gender expression. Also, we concluded with an example of a person born male that growing up identified as a woman. I didn’t say it back then, but I will now: the person from the example is a trans person.

What does it mean to be trans? Transgender (or simply trans) is anyone who has a gender identity that is different from the gender that they were assigned at birth. A trans woman is someone born male that identifies with being a woman.

We can also use the prefix cis to name a cisgender person: anyone who identifies exclusively with the gender that they were assigned at birth. For example, a cis woman is someone born female that identifies with being a woman. I’m a cis woman, for example.

Perhaps you’ve seen the terms cis and trans already. They come from Latin and mean "this side of" and "the other side of", respectively. Organic chemistry uses these terms to explain isomers (?).

In order to modify their gender, trans people might modify their bodies and hormones to change their sex (commonly known as a transexual person), or may not feel it’s necessary to intervene their bodies and want to be identified and recognized by others with a different gender (transgender). Anyhow, what’s important to know is that the term trans englobes people who weren’t assigned the gender they identify with, and wish to change it by either altering their appearance, their names, their pronouns, their clothes, etc.

Up until now, we know that people born unmistakenly male/female are called trans if they identify with a different gender. However… there are arrows that do not point to either man/woman. What are they?

I promise we’ll get there. First, we have to answer another question: what about intersex people, that we learned about in #23? Are they trans from the moment they’re born? Are they not cis at all?

Well, there are a few ways to answer. There seems to be a common agreement around the fact that the term cisgender does not account for intersex people’s lived experience recognizing that babies are assigned male or female at birth regardless of intersex status.

I consulted some intersex bloggers about it. Hida Viloria talks about the gender binary blind spot and how intersex people identify. Cary Gabriel Costello, for example, introduces in this post the term ipso gender:

A person who is born intersex, then medically and legally assigned to a binary sex, who then grows up to identify with that sex is ipso gender.

According to Cary, when speaking of intersex experience, here’s how we should use trans and cis:

A trans gender intersex person is one who gender transitions to the binary sex they were not forced into at birth. For intersex people, being born sex-intermediate, a cis gender identity would be a nonbinary identity of some sort.

Nonbinary identity? Does that exist?

It does :)

Understanding intersex has helped us replace the question marks in the diagram with a new term: Non-Binary. It is widely used to describe a gender identity that can not be categorized as masculine or feminine. Coming back to trans people, they can identify as male or female but may also identify outside this binary. Trans woman, trans man, trans non-binary:
